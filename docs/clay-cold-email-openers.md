# Clay Cold Email Openers — Example Bank

Use these as few-shot examples in the Clay AI column prompt. The model should match the TONE and STYLE of these examples, not copy the content.

**Voice:** Consultative — like an advisor who understands their world.
**Length:** 1-2 sentences max.
**Rules:** No exclamation marks. No "I saw your post." No "I hope this finds you well." No salesy language.

---

## Post-Based Openers (when a relevant LinkedIn post is found)

### Ops Managers / Directors

1. You made a great point about verification bottlenecks slowing down clear-to-close — it's something we hear from almost every ops team running more than 500 loans a month.

2. Your post about standardizing QC workflows got me thinking — a lot of the verification errors you mentioned tend to trace back to manual data entry between systems.

3. Interesting take on borrower experience being the next competitive advantage in lending. Most ops leaders I talk to are finding that the biggest friction point is still the back-and-forth on income documentation.

4. Your frustration with re-keying data resonated — one ops director I work with described it as "paying people to be copy machines," which is about right.

5. You raised a good question about balancing speed with compliance on verifications. The teams I see doing both well have automated the data transfer layer so processors aren't choosing between fast and accurate.

6. Your post about onboarding new processors hit on something we hear constantly — training someone to manually re-key verification data into Encompass takes weeks, and the error rate stays high for months.

7. The point you made about turn times being a retention issue is spot on. We've seen teams cut 2-3 days off their average just by removing the manual verification step from the processor's plate.

8. You mentioned struggling with paystub collection from borrowers — it's one of those problems that seems small until you multiply it across every file in your pipeline.

9. Your comments on vendor fatigue in mortgage tech were refreshing. Curious whether the verification side of your workflow has been part of that conversation, since that's where we tend to see the most manual lift.

10. The stat you shared about processor overtime during peak season tracks with what we're seeing — most of that extra time is spent on verification data entry and borrower follow-up, not actual underwriting decisions.

### VPs / C-Suite

11. Your post about margin compression in the current rate environment was sharp — a lot of the production leaders I talk to are finding that operational efficiency is the only lever they can actually control right now.

12. You made an interesting point about buyback risk being underpriced. The verification layer is usually where the data integrity issues start, especially when processors are manually entering income figures.

13. Your take on needing to do more with fewer processors is exactly what's driving the conversations I'm having with ops teams at similar-sized lenders.

14. The hiring challenge you described is real — and it's pushing a lot of lending leaders to rethink which parts of the workflow actually need a human in the loop.

15. You raised a good point about clear-to-close timelines being the hidden cost center. In our experience, verification is the step that creates the most unpredictable delays because it depends on borrower responsiveness.

16. Your comments about scaling without proportionally scaling headcount resonated. The verification and data entry layer is usually the first place where that math breaks down.

17. Interesting perspective on how GSE requirements are getting stricter while turn-time expectations keep shrinking. That tension is exactly why we built what we built.

18. Your post about the real cost of a loan defect was eye-opening. A lot of that risk originates at the re-keying step, where a transposed digit can cascade into a repurchase demand.

### Loan Processors

19. You captured something real in your post about the daily grind of chasing borrowers for documents — it's one of those tasks that everyone knows is broken but nobody prioritizes fixing.

20. Your post about wishing Encompass had better automation hit close to home for us. That gap between what Encompass does natively and what processors actually need is exactly where we focus.

21. The workaround you described for pulling paystub data manually is something we hear about constantly — it works, but it shouldn't take that many steps.

22. You made a good point about how much time gets lost switching between systems during verification. That context-switching cost adds up to hours per week across a team.

23. Your frustration with having to re-verify data you already verified once is valid — pre-closing reverification shouldn't mean redoing the entire process from scratch.

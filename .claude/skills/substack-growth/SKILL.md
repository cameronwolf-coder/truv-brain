# Substack Growth Framework

## Overview

A 3-phase framework for growing a personal essay Substack: **Position, Produce, Promote**. Adapted from Taylin Simmonds' methodology for Cameron's niche: personal essays about mental health, self-awareness, and figuring yourself out in your 30s. Not a business newsletter. Not advice content. Thinking out loud in public.

## Cameron's Positioning

> **Who reads this:** People in their late 20s/30s who are functional but exhausted. Holding jobs, maintaining friendships, but quietly drowning. The "I'm fine" crowd who know they're not.
>
> **What this is:** Personal essays that think out loud about mental health, identity, and what "getting better" actually looks like.
>
> **Why it's different:** Not a therapist. Not a wellness influencer. A guy in his 30s unlearning survival patterns, writing about it like he's telling a friend over beers. Specific details carry it. Receipts, not advice.

## When to Use

- Planning or auditing a Substack presence
- Generating essay ideas or building a content calendar
- Optimizing profile/bio/welcome email
- Developing an engagement and promotion strategy
- Figuring out what to write next

## The Framework

### Phase 1: Position (Who You Are & Why It Resonates)

For personal essay writers, positioning isn't about "helping" or "value propositions." It's about answering: **Why should someone follow your thinking?**

**Profile optimization checklist:**
- [ ] **Bio:** What you write about + the energy of it (not a resume). e.g. "Essays about mental health, identity, and what happens when you stop pretending you're fine."
- [ ] **Photo:** Authentic headshot. Not corporate, not curated.
- [ ] **Banner:** Something that signals tone. Personal, not branded.
- [ ] **Welcome email:** Brief, honest. Why you started writing. Link to 2-3 posts that represent what subscribers will get. No bonuses or lead magnets. Just: here's what I'm about.

### Phase 2: Produce (Write What's Actually Happening)

Your content naturally follows the **Living in Public** arc. Not building a business in public. Living a life in public.

**Three essay modes (rotate or blend):**

**Realizations** - Patterns you're noticing in yourself
- "I showed up to help someone move two weeks after neck surgery. Why?"
- "I spent a decade on meds before asking if they were working."
- The insight isn't advice. It's recognition.

**Stories** - Specific moments that carry weight
- The gun on the evening walk. The Ford Fusion in the garage. The birthday Caesar salad.
- Details > abstractions. Always.
- Let the reader find the meaning. Don't explain it for them.

**Updates** - Where you actually are right now
- What changed since last time. What didn't.
- Open questions you're sitting with.
- "For now" energy, not conclusions.

**Content cadence:**

| Format | Frequency | Notes |
|--------|-----------|-------|
| Notes | 2-5/day | Raw thoughts, reactions, questions. One or two sentences. |
| Essays | 1/week | Long-form, 1500+ words. The main thing. |
| Podcast | Optional | Only if it feels natural, not obligatory |

**Story arc:** Each essay should feel like an unfolding story. End with open questions, not answers. The reader should want to know what happens next in your life, not what tip comes next.

**Voice guard rails** (from Cameron's voice profile):
- Start in the middle. No throat-clearing.
- Short sentences hit. Fragments work.
- Specific numbers over adjectives.
- Vulnerable but never self-pitying. Own it.
- Humor as pressure release, even in heavy content.
- No em-dashes. No "Here's the thing." No motivational speaker energy.
- End with "for now" energy, not false certainty.

### Phase 3: Promote (Get Your Work Found)

Three traffic sources working simultaneously:

**1. Algorithm Traffic** (passive, slow)
- Platform distributes to engaged subscribers and similar readers
- Personal essays in the mental health/self-improvement space have strong recommendation potential on Substack
- Baseline visibility. Don't rely on this alone.

**2. Manual Traffic** (15-30 min/day)
- Comment thoughtfully on other personal essay writers' posts. Share how their piece connected to your own experience. Be a reader first.
- DM 5 new people daily. Genuine. Not "love your work, check out mine." Actually engage.
- Read and subscribe to Substacks in your space: mental health, personal essays, masculinity/vulnerability, sobriety/recovery adjacent, "figuring it out in your 30s" writers.
- Consistency matters: expect 4+ months before momentum builds.

**3. Collaboration Traffic** (highest leverage)
- Substack Lives with writers exploring similar themes
- Cross-recommendations with other personal essayists
- Guest posts or "conversations" with writers whose audiences overlap
- Target: writers at similar or slightly larger scale who write about mental health, identity, growth, honesty

## Quick-Start Daily Routine

```
Morning:  2-3 Notes (raw thoughts, a question, a reaction to something you read)
Midday:   15-30 min engagement (read other writers, comment genuinely, 5 DMs)
Evening:  Write. Even 20 minutes toward the weekly essay.
Weekly:   Publish 1 essay.
Monthly:  1 collaboration (Live, cross-post, conversation).
```

## Essay Idea Generation

When stuck, use these prompts:

**Pattern recognition:** "What's something I keep doing that I only recently noticed?"
**Specific moment:** "What happened this week that I'm still thinking about? Why?"
**Contradiction:** "What do I believe that contradicts how I actually behave?"
**Open question:** "What am I sitting with right now that I don't have an answer to?"
**Revisit:** "What did I write about before that I now see differently?"

The best essays come from the thing you can't stop thinking about. Not the thing you think you should write about.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Writing advice instead of experience | You're not a coach. Write what happened, not what others should do. |
| Over-explaining the meaning | Trust the reader. The specific detail carries the weight. |
| Polishing away the rawness | If it sounds like a TED talk, rewrite it. |
| Only posting essays, no Notes | Notes keep you visible between essays. Low effort, high signal. |
| Passive "post and pray" | 15-30 min/day engaging with other writers. Be a reader first. |
| Waiting for the perfect essay | Publish consistently. Imperfect and regular > perfect and sporadic. |
| Giving up before month 4 | Momentum is nonlinear. The first months feel like shouting into nothing. |
| Trying to be relatable instead of specific | "I was sad" < "I ate a Caesar salad alone on my birthday." Specificity IS relatability. |

## Audit Prompt

When auditing the Substack, evaluate:

1. **Position:** Does the bio signal personal essays, not business content? Does the welcome email feel like Cameron, not a funnel?
2. **Produce:** Is there a consistent weekly essay? Do Notes go out daily? Does the writing follow the voice profile (raw, specific, no polish)?
3. **Promote:** Is daily engagement happening? Are you reading and commenting on other writers? Any collaborations in the pipeline?

Rate each phase 1-5. The weakest phase is where to focus.
